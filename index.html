    About This · The Biorythm Tool

    The Honest Part First. Biorhythms are not science. Controlled studies comparing biorhythm predictions against athletic performance, accident rates, and cognitive test scores find the cycles perform no better than chance. This tool does not dispute that.

    The Company It Keeps. What biorhythms share is a long tradition with shaman tools that are equally "unscientific" and equally persistent across human cultures: tarot cards (14th-century Europe), the I Ching (China, ~1000 BCE), astrology (Babylonian in origin), numerology — and, in its modern form, psychotherapy. A therapist offers you a symbolic framework: the unconscious, attachment styles, cognitive distortions. You locate yourself within it. Something shifts. The framework does not need to be literally true to be useful. It needs to be coherent enough to hold your attention and open enough to receive projection.

    What Actually Happens. When you sit with a tarot spread, an I Ching hexagram, or a biorhythm chart and ask "what does this mean for me today?" — you are receiving information from your inside universe. You are performing an act of directed introspection. The symbol is a prompt. The insight comes from you. Carl Jung called this active imagination: give the unconscious a surface to project onto, and in reaching toward it the mind reveals what it already knew but had not yet articulated. The Rorschach inkblot works on the same principle. There is nothing in the blot. Everything you see in it is yours.

    Why a Sine Wave. The chart gives you a cycle to locate yourself within. "Am I in a high period or a low?" is a useful contemplative question — not because the math is tracking something real in your body, but because asking it builds a habit of self-monitoring. You check in. You pause. The sine wave is a language: a rising-and-falling shape the human mind finds intuitive. We already think in rhythms. The chart gives that intuition a form to inhabit.

    The Heads-Up Effect. When the chart tells you your emotional cycle is low today, something useful happens even before you check in with yourself: you are now watching. The reticular activating system — the brain's attention filter — has been handed a topic. For the rest of the day you will notice emotional moments, reactions, and moods that you would otherwise let pass unmarked. The observation is real. The insight that comes from it is real. The sine wave that prompted it does not need to be.

    This is what makes biorhythms — and tarot, and the I Ching, and a good therapy session — worth the time. Not the system. The attention the system recruits.

    The Three Lenses. The chart gives you three domains to watch — not three predictions, but three questions.

    • Physical —how does my body want to move today? Energy, appetite for effort, the quality of rest.
    • Emotional —how close to the surface is feeling today? High means open; low means quieter — not worse, just more interior.
    • Intellectual —where is my attention landing? Sharp and focused, or diffuse and wandering? Both are useful.

    You do not need to match the chart. You need only to ask the question — and pay attention to the answer that arrives.

    How to Use It. Treat the chart as a daily question, not a daily answer. Open it the way you might open a journal. Look at where the three waves sit today. Ask: does this match how I feel? If it does — stay with that. If it doesn't — that is equally interesting. You are giving your subconscious a structured prompt. The chart shows a high, a low, a crossing. You notice. You let that awareness land — and consciousness sharpens around it. That is enough.
